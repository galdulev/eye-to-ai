# The Operating System That Never Ran: A Review of *Scaling Good* by Gal Dulev

**Reviewed by Dr. Margaret Chen**
*Professor of Nonprofit Management, Stanford Graduate School of Business*
*Stanford Social Innovation Review, Spring 2026*

---

Gal Dulev's *Scaling Good* arrives with an ambitious promise: a unified, sequential framework — the "Impact Stack" — that maps the entire nonprofit organizational journey from program design through ecosystem change, drawing on technology-sector methodology to illuminate what the social sector has long struggled to systematize. The ambition alone merits attention, and in places, Dulev delivers. The "Dignity Test" — a deceptively simple heuristic for evaluating whether program design preserves beneficiary agency — is the book's most original contribution, and one I expect to see adopted widely. The four-type taxonomy of organizational debt (process, culture, knowledge, governance) gives leaders precise vocabulary for problems they have previously described only in terms of exhaustion. The chapter on the Founder Trap is among the finest treatments of founder-dependent organizations I have encountered in practitioner literature. These are real contributions. I want to be clear about that, because what follows is severe.

**1. The Book Fails Its Own Test**

*Scaling Good* is, at its core, an argument for evidence-based practice. Dulev insists that nonprofits must validate their program models before scaling, measure outcomes rather than count outputs, and resist the temptation to launch at full scale before testing. The book introduces over ten original frameworks, forty diagnostic tools, and a five-layer sequential model of organizational development. Not one of these has been tested. Not one is accompanied by outcome data. Not one is validated against a control group, a longitudinal cohort, or even a systematic case study. The book provides zero evidence that the Impact Stack works.

This is not a minor omission. It is a structural contradiction that undermines the entire intellectual project. Dulev asks nonprofit leaders to abandon intuition-driven practice and embrace rigorous validation — in a book that is itself intuition-driven practice with no rigorous validation. By the book's own standard, the Impact Stack is a program launched at full scale before achieving Program-Community Fit. The author has, to use his own terminology, shipped an MVP without running a single hypothesis test. The ethical consistency audit is devastating: the book scores 1.5 out of 7 against its own stated principles. No beneficiary voice appears in the text. No evidence of participatory design with nonprofit leaders is disclosed. The result is a book about listening that does not appear to have listened.

**2. The Debts the Book Does Not Acknowledge**

The most troubling scholarly problem is what goes uncited. Ann Mei Chang's *Lean Impact* (2018) applied Lean Startup methodology to the social sector comprehensively — the same core move that animates Dulev's Layer 1. Chang is not mentioned. Dan Pallotta's *Uncharitable* and his widely viewed TED talk dismantled the overhead myth years before Chapter 8 makes the same argument in the same terms. Pallotta is not cited. Kania and Kramer's collective impact framework predates and substantially overlaps the book's treatment of coalition-building in Chapter 16. IDEO's human-centered design toolkit prefigures the co-design methodology in Chapter 3. Mario Morino's *Leap of Reason* covers measurement and accountability in language Dulev's Layers 2 and 3 closely echo. None of these intellectual debts are acknowledged. The book reads as though these ideas were arrived at independently, which strains credulity. A work that demands transparency from the organizations it counsels must model that transparency in its own scholarship. The absence of a bibliography is not a stylistic choice; it is an accountability failure.

**3. The Architecture Contradicts Itself**

The book's central structural claim — that the five layers of the Impact Stack must be built sequentially, that "you can't skip ahead" — is contradicted by the book's own evidence. In the opening of Chapter 11, Dulev presents two organizations: one with strong outcomes but a weak narrative (broke), another with modest outcomes but a compelling story (funded at three times the rate). This is an example of an organization succeeding at Layer 4 without Layer 1. The book presents it as a problem to solve. It is also a disproof of the model's foundational constraint. More broadly, the dependency logic runs backward in at least two places: Layer 2 capabilities (measurement) are required to achieve Layer 1 (Program-Community Fit), since you cannot validate fit without feedback loops. And small organizations form coalitions (Layer 5) precisely *because* they lack growth engine resources (Layer 4), not as a product of them. Partnership substitutes for scale; it does not follow from it. Teach For America scaled massively without community co-design. Médecins Sans Frontières built a global reputation without formal outcome measurement for thirty-five years. The Bridgespan Group has documented that over ninety percent of large nonprofits raised the bulk of their funding from a single category — the opposite of the diversification Layer 4 prescribes. These are not edge cases. They are some of the most prominent organizations in the sector. A framework that cannot account for them is not universal; it is aspirational.

**4. Written for Some, Addressed to All**

The book claims to serve "every nonprofit leader." It does not. The practical guidance is calibrated for organizations with budgets between $500,000 and $5 million, staffs of five to thirty, in a growth phase hitting the limits of founder-centric management. For a two-person organization running on a single $80,000 grant, the Impact Stack is inaccessible — implementing it requires the very organizational capacity the book acknowledges most nonprofits lack. For a $50 million institution with a compliance department and a CFO, it is elementary. The bandwidth required to adopt even Layer 1 presupposes resources that the majority of nonprofits in the United States do not possess. Eleven coined concepts at a graduate reading level would exclude many of the community-based leaders the book's empathy passages invoke. The irony is precise: the book's central argument is that programs designed *for* people fail, while programs designed *with* people succeed. The book itself appears designed *for* nonprofit leaders, not *with* them.

**5. The Direction of the Lecture**

There is a question of posture that the book does not confront. Every comparison between the technology sector and the nonprofit sector runs in one direction: technology solved it, nonprofits haven't. Nine times, Dulev uses the construction "the nonprofit sector has no," "is terrible at," or "has never." There is no instance in which the nonprofit sector understood something before the technology sector did — not empathy, not community accountability, not the limits of scale, not the moral complexity of intervention. The prologue presents top-decile tech culture as the norm: collaborative, ego-free, data-driven. The phrase "without ego" is the most romanticized in the manuscript. Anyone who has spent time in Silicon Valley will recognize that ego is its defining currency. Surveillance capitalism, catastrophic product failures, diversity crises, and labor exploitation go unmentioned. The comparison is structurally unfair: best-case technology versus worst-case nonprofit. This would be a rhetorical problem in any book. In a book written by a technology professional lecturing a sector defined by chronic underfunding and moral complexity, it is something closer to a power problem.

**Verdict**

*Scaling Good* contains genuine insight trapped inside an unfinished intellectual project. Its best ideas — the Dignity Test, the organizational debt taxonomy, the Founder Trap analysis — deserve the audience the book seeks. But the framework that organizes them has not been tested, its key intellectual debts go unacknowledged, its structural logic is internally contradicted, its audience is narrower than it admits, and its tone reproduces the power asymmetry it claims to disrupt. The book argues, passionately and correctly, that good intentions are not enough. It then asks us to accept a comprehensive organizational framework on the strength of good intentions alone. Dulev should apply his own methodology: test the stack, cite the predecessors, name the boundaries, and return with evidence. The bones of an important book are here. But bones are not yet a body.
