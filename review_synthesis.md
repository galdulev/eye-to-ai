# Scaling Good — Synthesized Review Findings

**Source:** 11 parallel review steps
**Purpose:** Feed into Red Team Summary (Step 13), Revision Priority Matrix (Step 14), Executive Summary (Step 15)

## CATEGORY 1: CRITICAL ISSUES

Issues that undermine the book's credibility if not addressed before publication. A serious reviewer, competitor author, or hostile critic could use these to discredit the book.

---

### C1.1 — The book provides zero evidence that its own frameworks have been tested, while arguing for evidence-based practice.

- **Steps:** 7, 8
- **Severity:** CRITICAL — the single most damaging vulnerability
- **Detail:** The Ethical Consistency Audit (Step 7) scored the book 1.5/7 on its own principles. 10 named concepts, 40+ tools, zero validated outcomes. The book launches at full scale before validating — the exact failure mode it diagnoses in others. Step 8 found ~15-20 contentious claims with no evidence and ~25-30 plausible claims with no citations. A reviewer can summarize the book's problem in one sentence: "It advocates MVP thinking but skipped its own MVP."
- **Fix required:** Major addition. At minimum: (a) transparent disclosure that frameworks are proposed models, not validated systems, (b) 25-30 endnotes/references for key claims, (c) any pilot data, practitioner feedback, or field-testing results that exist. A "How This Book Was Built" section applying the book's own logic to itself would neutralize the critique.

### C1.2 — Amazon profitability claim is factually wrong.

- **Steps:** 2, 8
- **Severity:** CRITICAL — easy to verify, easy to weaponize
- **Detail:** Ch.8 states Amazon was "not profitable for its first twenty years." First full-year profit was 2003 (~9 years after founding). This is a >2x overstatement on a well-known, easily Googled fact. Any informed reader will catch it, and it casts doubt on all other claims.
- **Fix required:** Single sentence correction. Change "twenty years" to approximately nine years, or reframe around Amazon's deliberate reinvestment strategy.

### C1.3 — Ann Mei Chang's *Lean Impact* (2018) is not cited despite very high overlap.

- **Steps:** 3
- **Severity:** CRITICAL — risks appearing either ignorant of or deliberately ignoring the closest competitor
- **Detail:** Chang's book already applied Lean Startup to the social sector comprehensively. "Scaling Good" covers much of the same territory without acknowledgment. A reviewer familiar with both books could frame this as derivative rather than original. Dan Pallotta's *Uncharitable* (same core argument as Ch.8) and Kania & Kramer's Collective Impact framework (overlap with Ch.16) are also uncited.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. Add citations to Chang, Pallotta, Kania & Kramer, IDEO's human-centered design toolkit, and Morino's *Leap of Reason*. Position the book as a synthesis and unified framework, not an invention. ~2-3 paragraphs in introduction + scattered citations.

### C1.4 — No beneficiary voice appears anywhere in the book.

- **Steps:** 7, 4, 11
- **Severity:** CRITICAL — directly contradicts the book's core ethical argument
- **Detail:** The Dignity Test asks whether programs are designed WITH people. The Participatory Design chapter argues for co-creation. Yet zero beneficiary perspectives appear in the text. Step 7 scored Participatory Design at 0/1. Step 4 identified the irony: the book was designed FOR non-profit leaders, not WITH them.
- **Fix required:** Moderate addition. Include at least 3-5 direct beneficiary quotes or perspectives, ideally in the chapters on PCF (Ch.1), co-design (Ch.3), and the Dignity Test. Even a brief author's note acknowledging this gap and describing steps taken would help.

### C1.5 — 9 of 10 social-sector anecdotes are anonymous with no disclosure of composite status.

- **Steps:** 7, 2, 8
- **Severity:** CRITICAL — creates an unfalsifiable evidence base
- **Detail:** The book's evidence consists largely of anonymous stories that cannot be verified. The Nov 9, 2010 software bug story (Step 2) is unverifiable and likely composite. The housing program (22% → 61%), the $3M youth center, and other key cases are unattributed. Meanwhile, tech anecdotes use named companies (Amazon, Linux, etc.). This asymmetry undermines trust.
- **Fix required:** Moderate revision. For each anonymous anecdote, either: (a) name the organization with permission, (b) explicitly label as composite/illustrative, or (c) cite a published source for the underlying data. Add a note explaining the anonymization approach.

### C1.6 — The 5-layer "can't skip ahead" claim is contradicted by the book's own examples and by well-known real-world cases.

- **Steps:** 1, 9
- **Severity:** CRITICAL — the central structural claim has multiple visible counterexamples
- **Detail:** Step 1 found the book provides its own counter-example in Ch.11 (Org B succeeds at Layer 4 without Layer 1). Step 9 identified 7 strong real-world counter-examples: TFA scaled without community co-design, MSF built reputation without outcome measurement, GAVI thrived on funding concentration, PIH's founder-centricity was a superpower. The book also has circular dependencies (L1 requires L2 capabilities; L4 funding needed to build L1 and L3). Presenting the stack as an iron law when exceptions are abundant is a credibility risk.
- **Fix required:** Moderate rewrite of framing. Reposition from "strict prerequisites" to "strong mutual reinforcements with a general bottom-up bias." Add a "Where This Framework Breaks" section or sidebar acknowledging boundary conditions. Distinguish greenfield ideal sequence from practical reality for existing orgs.

### C1.7 — Racial equity, DEI, and systemic racism are entirely absent.

- **Steps:** 10
- **Severity:** CRITICAL — conspicuous omission in 2026 for a book about social-sector effectiveness
- **Detail:** The words "racism," "DEI," "equity," and "anti-racism" never appear. For a book addressing the social sector — where racial disparities are central to program design, hiring, funding, and community trust — this absence will be noticed and criticized. It is especially glaring in chapters on co-design, measurement, and systems change.
- **Fix required:** Modest additions distributed across existing chapters. Not a new chapter, but acknowledgment in PCF/co-design (racialized empathy gaps), measurement (disaggregated data), organizational debt (exclusionary culture debt), and systems change (structural racism as root cause). ~5-8 paragraphs total.

## CATEGORY 2: SIGNIFICANT GAPS

Issues a thoughtful reader would notice and flag. These don't destroy credibility but weaken the book's impact and authority.

---

### S2.1 — Scale mismatch: book claims to serve "every non-profit leader" but is calibrated for $500K-$5M orgs.

- **Steps:** 4
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Detail:** Most advice is impossible for 2-person organizations and elementary for $50M organizations. The framework requires significant organizational capacity to implement — the very capacity the book acknowledges most orgs don't have.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. Explicitly name the sweet-spot audience (ED or senior leader, $500K-$5M budget, 5-30 staff, growth phase). Add scale-specific guidance or triage notes for smaller and larger orgs. ~3-5 paragraphs in introduction.

### S2.2 — Tech condescension: every comparison runs one direction.

- **Steps:** 11, 4
- **Severity:** HIGH — will alienate the primary audience
- **Detail:** 9 instances of "the non-profit sector has no / is terrible at / has never / needs" construction. No instance where "the non-profit sector understood X before tech did." Comparison is structurally unfair: best-case tech vs. worst-case non-profit. Tech knowledge is implicitly elevated; non-profit practitioners positioned as behind, not as constrained.
- **Fix required:** Moderate revision. Rewrite the 9 flagged instances. Add 3-5 reverse examples (areas where nonprofits lead). Acknowledge structural constraints (chronic underfunding) rather than framing gaps as organizational failings. Ensure bidirectional respect.

### S2.3 — Tech romanticization: Silicon Valley presented without its failures.

- **Steps:** 11, 6
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Detail:** Prologue presents top-decile tech culture as norm. "Without ego" is the most romanticized phrase. No mention of surveillance capitalism, failed products, diversity failures, labor exploitation. The book cherry-picks tech's self-image while stripping away its track record. Tech readers (Step 6) will find this naive.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. 2-3 paragraphs acknowledging tech's own failures: high startup failure rates, ethical controversies, Agile adoption challenges. This actually strengthens the argument — if tech has these tools AND still fails often, the tools must be adapted thoughtfully.

### S2.4 — Organizational Debt framing risks victim-blaming.

- **Steps:** 5, 6
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Detail:** The veteran reviewer (Step 5) identified this sharply: "The debt exists because we are chronically underfunded. Calling this 'organizational debt' is victim-blaming with nicer vocabulary." Step 6 noted the concept imports only the negative framing — deliberate debt as strategy is absent.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. Add acknowledgment that much organizational debt is externally imposed by chronic underfunding, not internally generated by poor management. Introduce "deliberate debt" as a legitimate strategic choice. ~2-3 paragraphs in Ch.7.

### S2.5 — "Prologue thirty percent" underpay claim needs sourcing; documented gap is 4-18%.

- **Steps:** 2, 8
- **Severity:** MODERATE-HIGH
- **Detail:** The "thirty percent" figure in the Prologue sets the emotional tone of the book. If it's significantly overstated (documented data shows 4-18% depending on role), it undermines trust early.
- **Fix required:** Single paragraph. Either source the 30% figure with specific data, or adjust to the documented range with citation.

### S2.6 — The Generosity Paradox is asserted without evidence and has a strong counter-example.

- **Steps:** 6, 8, 9
- **Severity:** MODERATE-HIGH
- **Detail:** Ch.15 claims open-source sharing creates a paradox where giving away increases impact. Step 6 notes zero marginal cost of software doesn't transfer to program replication. Step 8 flags "an observable pattern, supported by evidence" with no evidence provided. Step 9 identifies the Montessori counter-example where IP protection led to better outcomes than diluted open sharing.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. Provide actual evidence or case studies. Acknowledge boundary conditions (intervention complexity, quality control). Address the zero-marginal-cost distinction. ~2-3 paragraphs.

### S2.7 — Staff mental health, burnout, and compassion fatigue receive no substantive treatment.

- **Steps:** 10, 4
- **Severity:** MODERATE-HIGH
- **Detail:** Mentioned in passing but no framework, tools, or meaningful discussion. Compassion fatigue and vicarious trauma are absent. For a book about organizational effectiveness in the social sector, this is a notable gap — burnout is a primary driver of turnover and organizational dysfunction.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. ~2-3 paragraphs in the organizational culture or HR chapters. Could integrate into the Organizational Debt taxonomy (health debt as a fifth type, or subset of culture debt).

### S2.8 — MVP in grant-funded reality is unaddressed.

- **Steps:** 5
- **Severity:** MODERATE-HIGH
- **Detail:** The veteran reviewer's sharpest practical criticism: "My budget is 97% committed to deliverables specified by contracts and grants." MVP requires slack resources for experimentation. Most nonprofits have zero slack. The book doesn't address how to run MVPs within the constraints of restricted funding.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. ~1-2 paragraphs acknowledging restricted-funding constraints and offering practical workarounds (embedding pilots within existing programs, using unrestricted funds, negotiating flexibility with funders).

### S2.9 — Beneficiary agency (Part One) disappears in donor-centric Part Four.

- **Steps:** 1
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Detail:** The book champions beneficiary-centered design in early chapters but pivots to donor-centric language and strategies in Part Four (funding). The tension between "design with beneficiaries" and "tell stories that move donors" is unresolved.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. Acknowledge the tension explicitly. Show how beneficiary agency can be maintained within fundraising narratives. ~1-2 paragraphs in Ch.11-12.

### S2.10 — Observability chapter is imprecise for tech readers.

- **Steps:** 6
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Detail:** "The chapter describes monitoring and calls it observability. For tech readers, the imprecise use of a precise term creates a credibility problem." Observability has a specific technical meaning (ability to infer internal states from external outputs) distinct from monitoring (watching known metrics).
- **Fix required:** Single paragraph. Either use the term precisely or acknowledge the adaptation.

### S2.11 — Volunteer management is ignored.

- **Steps:** 10
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Detail:** Volunteers are the primary workforce of many nonprofits. The organizational chapters focus entirely on paid staff, missing recruitment, retention, training, and motivation of volunteers.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. ~2-3 paragraphs in the team/operations chapters.

### S2.12 — International context is absent; 95%+ of examples are American.

- **Steps:** 10
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Detail:** The book's frameworks may or may not apply in different legal, cultural, and funding environments. No acknowledgment of this limitation.
- **Fix required:** Single paragraph acknowledgment, ideally in the introduction. Could note this as a boundary condition.

### S2.13 — Midwestern state / 2019 date for child welfare algorithm is likely inaccurate.

- **Steps:** 2
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Detail:** Resembles Allegheny County AFST (launched 2016, Pennsylvania — not midwestern) or Illinois case. Either incorrect details or composite without disclosure.
- **Fix required:** Single sentence correction or composite disclosure.

### S2.14 — Empathy Gap Audit lacks measurement rigor.

- **Steps:** 6
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Detail:** Tech reviewer noted: "The four questions are discovery heuristics, not fit metrics. Where's the measurement apparatus?" For a book arguing for data-driven practice, the primary diagnostic tool is qualitative and subjective.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. Either add quantitative indicators that signal PCF or acknowledge the Audit as a qualitative starting point that feeds into quantitative measurement (Layer 2).

### S2.15 — Legal/regulatory constraints are absent.

- **Steps:** 10
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Detail:** 501(c)(3) lobbying limits, fiduciary duty, compliance requirements — all shape what organizations can do but are never mentioned.
- **Fix required:** Single paragraph acknowledging regulatory constraints as a boundary condition on framework application.

### S2.16 — Advocacy-first organizations are not addressed by the framework.

- **Steps:** 1
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Detail:** Organizations whose primary activity is policy advocacy rather than direct service don't fit neatly into the Impact Stack. PCF, MVP, and outcomes measurement all assume a service-delivery model.
- **Fix required:** Single paragraph acknowledging this boundary condition, or an adaptation note for advocacy organizations.

### S2.17 — "Effectiveness is the highest form of compassion" presented as self-evident truth.

- **Steps:** 11
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Detail:** This is the book's tagline/thesis. It's a contentious philosophical claim (it privileges measurable outcomes over solidarity, presence, accompaniment, and bearing witness). Presenting it without engagement with counterarguments weakens it.
- **Fix required:** Single paragraph acknowledging the philosophical counterposition — that compassion sometimes means presence without measurable effect.

### S2.18 — Agile adoption risk for non-technical organizations is underexplored.

- **Steps:** 5, 6
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Detail:** Both reviewers flagged this. Agile failure rate is high even in tech. For nonprofits with no technical background, cargo-cult adoption risk is substantial. The veteran noted: "My staff's sprint gets interrupted every day by a client in crisis."
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. ~1-2 paragraphs on realistic Agile adaptation for disruption-heavy environments. Acknowledge partial adoption as valid.

### S2.19 — Author never expresses doubt, personal failure, or uncertainty.

- **Steps:** 11
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Detail:** The voice becomes preachy in the second half. Humor is almost entirely absent. No personal vulnerability or admission of being wrong. This creates emotional distance from readers who live with uncertainty daily.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. 2-3 moments of authorial vulnerability scattered through the text. Places where the author tried something and it didn't work, or where the author's own thinking evolved.

## CATEGORY 3: OPPORTUNITIES

Places where the book could be much stronger with relatively modest additions or revisions. These are upside improvements.

---

### O3.1 — Add a "Where This Framework Breaks" section.

- **Steps:** 9, 1
- **Severity/Impact:** HIGH upside
- **Detail:** Step 9 identified 7 strong counter-examples that reveal boundary conditions rather than weaken the thesis. Engaging with them openly (TFA, MSF, PIH, Montessori, Against Malaria Foundation, GAVI, Habitat) would dramatically increase the book's intellectual credibility and preempt criticism.
- **Fix required:** New section or extended sidebar. ~3-5 pages. Could be a chapter coda, an appendix, or woven into individual chapters.

### O3.2 — Add 25-30 endnotes/references to transform the evidence base.

- **Steps:** 8, 2
- **Severity/Impact:** HIGH upside — modest effort, massive credibility gain
- **Detail:** Step 8 concluded: "A modest endnotes section would transform credibility." Most claims are directionally correct but unattributed. Simply adding sources for the 25-30 most important claims would shift the book from "interesting argument" to "credible reference."
- **Fix required:** Research and compilation. ~5-10 pages of endnotes.

### O3.3 — Position the book explicitly as a synthesis, crediting intellectual debts.

- **Steps:** 3
- **Severity/Impact:** HIGH upside
- **Detail:** The book's genuine originality (Dignity Test, Org Debt taxonomy, sequential stack architecture, 37+ diagnostic tools) is strongest when framed as building on existing work. The proposed UVP: "No existing book provides a single, integrated, sequential framework covering the entire nonprofit organizational stack — from program design through measurement, operations, funding, and ecosystem — using a consistent vocabulary adapted from technology practice, with practical diagnostic tools at every layer."
- **Fix required:** Modest additions. ~2-3 paragraphs in introduction positioning the contribution. Scattered citations throughout.

### O3.4 — Add reverse examples: areas where nonprofits lead and tech lags.

- **Steps:** 11, 4
- **Severity/Impact:** HIGH upside — would transform tone
- **Detail:** No instance in the book where "the non-profit sector understood X before tech did." Adding 3-5 reverse examples (e.g., community organizing, stakeholder management, operating under resource constraints, ethical frameworks for vulnerable populations) would rebalance the power dynamic and earn trust with the primary audience.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. ~3-5 paragraphs distributed across chapters.

### O3.5 — Name the sweet-spot audience explicitly and add scale triage.

- **Steps:** 4
- **Severity/Impact:** MODERATE-HIGH upside
- **Detail:** The book's ideal reader is identifiable: ED or senior leader, $500K-$5M budget, 5-30 staff, hitting the ceiling of founder-centric informal operations. Naming this explicitly manages expectations and paradoxically broadens appeal (readers self-select more accurately).
- **Fix required:** Single paragraph in introduction + brief scale-adaptation notes per layer.

### O3.6 — Break structural formula in later chapters.

- **Steps:** 11
- **Severity/Impact:** MODERATE upside
- **Detail:** Every chapter follows: Anecdote → Diagnosis → Tech bridge → New concept → Implementation → Call to action. This becomes predictable by Part Four. The best chapters break formula (Ch.10 Founder Trap, Ch.17 Systems Change). Later chapters could vary structure to maintain energy.
- **Fix required:** Moderate revision of 3-4 chapters in Parts Four and Five.

### O3.7 — Add moments of authorial vulnerability and humor.

- **Steps:** 11
- **Severity/Impact:** MODERATE upside
- **Detail:** The voice currently lacks self-doubt or humor. Adding 2-3 moments where the author was wrong, struggled, or learned something unexpected would create emotional connection and model the intellectual honesty the book advocates.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. 2-3 paragraphs scattered through the text.

### O3.8 — Introduce "deliberate debt" as a strategic concept.

- **Steps:** 6
- **Severity/Impact:** MODERATE upside
- **Detail:** Tech readers know that not all debt is bad — deliberate debt taken knowingly for speed is different from accidental debt from negligence. Adding this distinction strengthens the Organizational Debt framework and preempts the "victim-blaming" criticism.
- **Fix required:** Single paragraph in Ch.7.

### O3.9 — Address the "stop what doesn't work" vs. making payroll tension.

- **Steps:** 5, 1
- **Severity/Impact:** MODERATE upside
- **Detail:** The veteran reviewer surfaced the sharpest practical tension: "I have had to choose between telling a funder the truth and making payroll. Both times I chose payroll." The book also has an internal tension between "the food line is essential" and "stop what doesn't work." Engaging with this honestly would deepen the book.
- **Fix required:** Modest addition. ~1-2 paragraphs acknowledging the ethical bind of dependency.

### O3.10 — Fix "Linux powers most supercomputers" to "all."

- **Steps:** 2
- **Severity/Impact:** LOW — but a free credibility upgrade
- **Detail:** 100% of the TOP500 supercomputers run Linux. "Most" undersells the point.
- **Fix required:** Single word change.

### O3.11 — Consolidate Ch.13 and Ch.14 or differentiate more clearly.

- **Steps:** 1
- **Severity/Impact:** LOW-MODERATE
- **Detail:** Ch.14 feels like a subset of Ch.13. The distinction is unclear.
- **Fix required:** Either merge or sharpen the differentiation. Moderate structural edit.

### O3.12 — Rework Ch.6 AI content integration.

- **Steps:** 1
- **Severity/Impact:** LOW-MODERATE
- **Detail:** AI content feels bolted-on rather than integrated into the chapter's argument.
- **Fix required:** Moderate rewrite of one chapter section.

## CATEGORY 4: STRENGTHS

What works well and must be preserved in any revision. These are the book's assets.

---

### ST4.1 — The Dignity Test is genuinely original and powerful.

- **Steps:** 3, 5, 6, 11
- **Detail:** Identified as the book's strongest claim to originality across every review. The veteran reviewer: "Simple, devastating question. We failed. We're working on it." The tech reviewer: "simple, memorable, devastating." Step 3 confirmed no prior work frames it this way.
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT. This is the book's signature contribution. Do not dilute with caveats.

### ST4.2 — The Founder Trap chapter (Ch.10) is the emotional and analytical peak.

- **Steps:** 5, 6, 11
- **Detail:** Both the veteran and tech reviewers singled it out. "Best I've read anywhere" (veteran). "Most emotionally resonant chapter" (tech). Step 11 identified it as one of the chapters that breaks formula most effectively.
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT. Could be the excerpt chapter for reviews/publicity.

### ST4.3 — The Overhead Myth chapter (Ch.8) is powerful and well-argued.

- **Steps:** 5, 6
- **Detail:** Veteran: "Would photocopy and leave on every foundation program officer's desk." Tech: "outstanding." The systemic critique of the overhead ratio is the book's strongest policy argument.
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT. Fix the Amazon factual error but preserve everything else.

### ST4.4 — The core thesis — "Effectiveness is the highest form of compassion" — is memorable and motivating.

- **Steps:** 6
- **Detail:** Tech reviewer: "I'd put it on the wall." This is the book's elevator pitch and it works.
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT the thesis itself. Add a brief philosophical acknowledgment of the counterposition (S2.17) without undermining the core claim.

### ST4.5 — The Organizational Debt taxonomy (four types: process, culture, knowledge, governance) is a valuable original contribution.

- **Steps:** 3, 11
- **Detail:** Step 3 identified this as one of the book's genuinely original elements. Step 11 rated it a "valuable adaptation with new taxonomy." The four-type classification gives practitioners a diagnostic vocabulary they currently lack.
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT. Strengthen with "deliberate debt" addition (O3.8) and structural-cause acknowledgment (S2.4).

### ST4.6 — The 37+ diagnostic tools in Appendix C provide practical, actionable value.

- **Steps:** 3
- **Detail:** No competing book offers this density of practical tools. This is the book's primary functional differentiator for practitioners.
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT. Consider adding a note about which tools have been field-tested vs. which are proposed.

### ST4.7 — The sequential 5-layer stack architecture is a genuinely novel organizational model.

- **Steps:** 3
- **Detail:** No existing work argues this specific integrated sequence. The architecture is the book's structural innovation.
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT the architecture itself. Soften the "can't skip" rigidity (C1.6) while preserving the insight that layers reinforce each other bottom-up.

### ST4.8 — Specific emotional passages are exceptional.

- **Steps:** 11, 5
- **Detail:** The Kulan opening, medical clown passage, housing program narrative, founder portrait, and food line passage are identified as the book's strongest emotional moments. The systems change food-line passage drew this from the veteran: "I know that food bank. I've been that food bank."
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT. These are the book's emotional anchors. Do not cut or shorten them.

### ST4.9 — Program-Community Fit and Organizational Debt are excellent tech-to-nonprofit translations.

- **Steps:** 4
- **Detail:** Among the 11 coined concepts, these two were rated as the strongest translations — concepts that genuinely illuminate nonprofit reality through a tech lens without distortion.
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT.

### ST4.10 — The consistent tech-to-nonprofit translation vocabulary across the full stack is unique.

- **Steps:** 3
- **Detail:** No other book maintains a consistent adapted vocabulary from program design through ecosystem-level strategy. This is what makes the book a "unified operating system" rather than a collection of essays.
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT the consistency. Improve individual translations where weak (Observability, Agile — see S2.10, S2.18).

### ST4.11 — The Prologue's empathetic framing sets a strong emotional foundation.

- **Steps:** 4, 11
- **Detail:** The Prologue effectively establishes the author's respect for nonprofit work and the emotional reality of the sector. Step 4 noted the empathy gap widens in later chapters — the Prologue shows the author can do this well.
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT. Use the Prologue's tone as a benchmark for revising later chapters that drift toward condescension.

### ST4.12 — Best chapters handle complexity with nuance.

- **Steps:** 11
- **Detail:** MVP ethical guardrails, algorithmic bias discussion, overhead myth systemic critique, and systems change tension (food line passage) all demonstrate the author's ability to hold complexity. These sections prove the author CAN do nuance — the book just doesn't do it consistently.
- **Preservation note:** PROTECT. Use these as models for revising sections that oversimplify (tech/nonprofit binary, "can't skip" rigidity).
