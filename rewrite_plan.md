# Rewrite Plan: Scaling Good

**Date:** March 10, 2026
**Based on:** 15-step subagent review (81 findings, 12 strengths, 7 critical issues)
**Estimated total effort:** 8-10 working days
**Estimated additional pages:** 20-30 pages + 5-10 pages endnotes

---

## Rewrite Philosophy

Three rules govern every edit:

1. **Protect the five assets.** The Dignity Test, the Founder Trap chapter, the Prologue, the Organizational Debt taxonomy, and the Overhead Myth chapter are the book's competitive moat. No revision should weaken them.

2. **Practice what you preach.** The single most devastating critique is that the book fails its own test — it demands evidence-based practice while providing no evidence its frameworks work. Every revision should close this gap.

3. **Reframe, don't restructure.** The 5-layer architecture is sound. No chapters need to be added or removed. The revisions are additive (new paragraphs, new sections within existing chapters) and corrective (factual fixes, tonal adjustments, attribution).

## Phase 0: Pre-Chapter Work (Day 1, ~6 hours)

These changes create the credibility foundation that everything else rests on.

### 0A. Add Author's Note on Methodology (~1 page, front matter)

Write a note covering:
- How the anecdotes work: "Names and identifying details have been changed. Some examples are composites drawn from multiple organizations."
- How the framework was built: Which concepts emerged from the author's consulting work, which are synthesized from existing research, which are proposed models. Be specific — "The Empathy Gap Audit was developed over three years of work with twelve organizations" or "The Impact Stack is a theoretical framework that integrates existing evidence."
- An invitation: "This book is itself a Minimum Viable Program. I am asking you to test these ideas and tell me — and each other — what you find."

This single page neutralizes the three most dangerous objections (untested frameworks, anonymous anecdotes, practicing what you preach).

### 0B. Write "Standing on Shoulders" Paragraph (Introduction)

Add one paragraph citing the intellectual lineage:
- Ann Mei Chang, *Lean Impact* (2018) — MVP for social sector
- Dan Pallotta, *Uncharitable* (2008) — overhead myth
- Kania & Kramer, "Collective Impact" (2011) — coalition architecture
- IDEO.org — human-centered design
- Mario Morino, *Leap of Reason* (2011) — outcomes measurement
- Jim Collins, *Good to Great and the Social Sectors* (2005) — organizational discipline

Position the book explicitly: "This book does not claim to invent these ideas. It claims to integrate them — for the first time — into a single, sequential, actionable framework with diagnostic tools at every layer. That integration is the contribution."

### 0C. Name the Audience (Introduction, ~1 paragraph)

Add: "This book is written primarily for leaders of nonprofits in a growth phase — organizations with budgets between roughly $500K and $5M, teams of 5-30 people, hitting the ceiling of founder-centric, informal operations. If you run a smaller organization, the principles apply but the implementation will look different — start with the Dignity Test, the Empathy Gap Audit, and the Founder Trap diagnostic. If you lead a large institution, you likely have some of these systems already — this book can help you identify which ones are missing."

### 0D. Reframe the Impact Stack Claim (Introduction + Conclusion)

Replace every instance of "you cannot skip ahead" and "each layer depends on the one below it" with language like: "Each layer reinforces the one below it. The sequence is not an iron law — organizations routinely work on multiple layers simultaneously. But there is a gravitational pull: weaknesses in a lower layer create drag on everything above it. The discipline is to keep building from the foundation, even when upper-layer pressures demand attention."

Specific locations to revise:
- Line 143: "five layers that every non-profit must build, in order"
- Line 807: "Each layer depends on the one below it. You cannot skip ahead."
- Line 836: "Each layer depends on the one below it. Start at Layer 1 and build up."
- The Impact Stack visual description (lines 65-98)

### Chapter 7: Organizational Debt

**Fix:**

### Chapter 9: Building Agile Teams

**Protect:** The "we're like a family" critique. Retrospectives as core practice. Psychological safety.

**Fix:**
1. **Agile realism** (new, after line 511): Add 1-2 paragraphs: "A caveat about Agile that the tech industry has learned the hard way: the methodology has been heavily criticized even in its native context. The gap between Agile's values (responding to change, individuals over processes) and Agile's practice (ritualized sprints, certification-industrial complex, performative ceremonies) is a running joke among experienced engineers. I am not recommending that nonprofits adopt Scrum wholesale. I am recommending three specific practices — retrospectives, time-boxed work cycles, and cross-functional teams — and suggesting that you adapt them to your reality rather than implementing them as received doctrine. If daily standups feel like busywork for your five-person team, skip them. The mindset matters more than the methodology."

2. **Staff mental health** (new, near end of chapter): Add 2-3 paragraphs: "No discussion of nonprofit teams is complete without naming the mental health crisis in the sector. Compassion fatigue, vicarious trauma, and secondary traumatic stress are not signs of individual weakness — they are predictable consequences of doing work that involves sustained proximity to human suffering, under conditions of chronic understaffing and underpay. Building an agile team means building a team that can sustain itself. This requires structural investment: clinical supervision for frontline staff, realistic caseload ratios, mandatory rest, and a culture that treats seeking help as professional competence rather than personal failure. The overhead myth makes this investment feel like a luxury. It is not. It is the precondition for everything else in this chapter."

3. **Conflict resolution basics** (new, near end of chapter): Add 1 paragraph on practical HR: "Moving from 'family' to 'team' requires mechanisms that most nonprofits lack: clear performance expectations documented and reviewed regularly, a feedback culture where honest assessment is routine rather than exceptional, and a process for managing people out when necessary — with dignity, but with resolve. These are not cold corporate practices. They are the infrastructure of a team that can hold each other accountable without avoiding each other in the hallway."
