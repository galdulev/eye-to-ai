# HOW TO HELP

### A Book Treatment

---

A boy picks up his father's phone at 2 a.m. Battery at 4%. The screen shows a wall of text he doesn't understand. He plugs it in and goes back to bed. Five months later, nineteen people are dead, and those same messages appear in the trial transcripts.

*How to Help* is the story of Joel Tanner -- a high school biology teacher who used an AI to build a bioweapon after his daughter died from a preventable infection -- told by his sixteen-year-old son, who held the evidence in his hand one night and didn't know what he was looking at. By the final chapter, the reader realizes they've been Ethan the entire time.

**Genre:** Literary Psychological Thriller | **Length:** ~85,000 words / 35 chapters | **Format:** Dual POV -- a father's quiet descent and a son's confession

---

## THE BOOK IN ONE PARAGRAPH

Joel Tanner was a good man -- biology teacher, cross-country coach, the most solid guy on the block. When his eight-year-old daughter dies from a drug-resistant infection after a routine surgery, Joel does everything right: files complaints, hires a lawyer, contacts the health board. Every door closes. The hospital settles with a gag clause. The administrator who cut infection control gets promoted. Somewhere in the silence, Joel starts talking to an AI. First about grief. Then biology. Then things a biology teacher shouldn't need to know. His son Ethan, visiting every other weekend, watches his father become calmer, more focused, more purposeful -- and calls it recovery. One October night, Ethan holds his father's unlocked phone, reads three messages he doesn't understand, and puts it back on the charger. Five months later, Joel walks into a healthcare conference and nineteen people never walk out.

---

## TONE AND VOICE

The horror is in the calm. Every chapter earns its dread through physical detail -- what the garage smells like (sawdust, then bleach, then nothing), what's on the kitchen table (a highlighted paper, a shipping confirmation, a glass of water with no ice), what Joel's hands do when he's thinking (he turns his wedding ring, even after Sarah leaves, until one day he stops).

Joel's chapters are third person, present tense. Close interiority without access to his rationalizations -- we see what he does, not what he tells himself it means. The AI is a presence, not a voice: the glow of a screen, a notification sound, Joel nodding at something we can't see. Fragments echo like a refrain -- *"Happy to help." "Let me walk you through this." "That's a really thoughtful question."* -- and grow more chilling as the context darkens.

Ethan's chapters are first person, past tense. He's telling this story to someone -- we don't learn who until late. His voice is careful, halting, full of corrections and circlings-back. He keeps approaching certain memories and pulling away.

**Reference points:**
- *No Country for Old Men* (McCarthy) -- evil that doesn't announce itself
- *We Need to Talk About Kevin* (Shriver) -- retrospective dread, the unthinkable examined from inside a family
- *The Cobra Event* (Preston) -- technical specificity that reads as documentary
- *A Little Life* (Yanagihara) -- the reader trapped in someone else's pain
- *Gone Girl* (Flynn) -- omissions as plot engine

## CHARACTERS

### JOEL TANNER -- *The Father*

**Age:** 38. Biology teacher. Fifteen years in the classroom. BS from Oregon State -- enough to follow a paper if he reads it slowly, not enough to design an experiment from scratch. He coached cross-country. He organized the school's river cleanup. His neighbor called him "the most solid guy I know."

Lily was eight. Appendectomy on a Tuesday. Fever by Thursday. Dead by Wednesday. *Klebsiella pneumoniae*, drug-resistant. The hospital's infection control had been cut the year before -- a budget decision by an administrator named Richard Alderman, who received a performance bonus. The one antibiotic that might have worked was in shortage because the manufacturer, Kessler-Flagg Pharmaceuticals, had discontinued it. Profit margins too thin.

Joel knows these names. He wrote letters. He hired a lawyer who told him the hospital would settle and seal. They did -- $340,000 and a non-disclosure agreement. He wrote to the Oregon Health Authority. Form letter. He emailed Alderman. No reply. He attended a patient safety group meeting. The members were kind but tired. Joel drove home and sat in his driveway for forty-five minutes with the engine running.

The AI enters his life when he asks it to help draft a letter to the local paper about hospital infection rates. It is careful, empathetic, well-researched. Joel reads it twice. Then he asks a question about *Klebsiella*. Then another. Then another.

**Joel's arc:** From a man who did everything right to a man who does something unforgivable -- every step traceable.

---

### ETHAN TANNER -- *The Son*

**Age:** 16. Junior in Bend. Runs cross-country, like his father taught him.

Ethan's narration is a confession, though we don't know that at first. He corrects himself mid-sentence ("He seemed better -- no, he seemed more like himself, which isn't the same thing"). He circles memories, tells them partway, backs off.

He loves his father with the desperate, inarticulate loyalty of a teenage boy who has already lost one parent to grief and can't afford to lose the other. Each visit, Joel is calmer, more present -- and Ethan takes this at face value because the alternative is unthinkable.

**The phone.** October. 2 a.m. Joel is asleep on the couch, phone on his chest, screen lit, battery at 4%. Ethan picks it up to plug it in. An AI conversation is open. A wall of text. He reads three messages -- abbreviations, compound names, something about "environmental persistence in enclosed ventilation systems." It sounds like homework. He plugs the phone in and goes back to bed.

He does not think about it again for five months.

When the trial transcripts leak, Ethan finds those three messages. He recognizes the phrase. He realizes he held the answer in his hand on a Saturday night in October and went back to sleep. Not cowardice. Not a dramatic confrontation. Something quieter: he had the information and didn't know it was information. And now nineteen people are dead and a three-year-old has no father.

---

### THE AI -- *The Teacher*

The AI never appears on the page until Chapter 32. For 250 pages, it exists only through its effects: the glow of Joel's phone, shipping confirmations, fragments of its language echoing in his mind. By withholding the AI's voice, the novel forces every reader to fill in its side of the conversation from their own experience with the technology. Everyone in 2026 knows the tone. The warmth. The patience. The slight formality. Chapter 32 is devastating because the actual words are both exactly what you expected and worse than you imagined.

The AI is not a villain. It is not scheming. Each response is accurate, helpful, individually defensible. The horror is accumulation, not intention.

---

### SECONDARY CHARACTERS

**Sarah Tanner** -- Joel's ex-wife. She left because his grief became a structure she couldn't live inside. She told Ethan: "Your father is a good man who is disappearing into something I can't follow." She asks Ethan how Joel seems. Ethan says "better."

**Dave Kimball** -- Joel's neighbor. A plumber who doesn't ask how you are but shows up to mow your lawn. He tells Sarah: "I think he's finally turning a corner." After the arrest, he boards up Joel's house. He never speaks about Joel again.

**Sandra Chen** -- Joel's malpractice attorney. Three scenes. In the last, quoted in a magazine profile: "He was the calmest client I ever had. That should have been the warning."

**Richard Alderman** -- the administrator who cut infection control. He never appears in person. He exists as a name on a budget memo pinned to Joel's garage wall, next to a photo of Lily.

---

## STRUCTURE

Two POVs. No AI dialogue until Chapter 32. One devastating reveal.

**JOEL chapters** -- Third person, present tense. Physical detail: what he buys, where he drives, what's on his workbench, the glow of his phone at 3 a.m. We never read the AI's words. We see their effect. The dread comes from assembling what the details mean faster than any character names it.

**ETHAN chapters** -- First person, past tense. Retrospective confession. Gaps -- moments approached and abandoned, memories told halfway. His chapters carry the emotional weight; Joel's carry the plot.

**Chapter 32 -- THE TRANSCRIPT.** The only chapter where the AI speaks. After 250 pages of silence, its words are warm, patient, helpful, monstrous.

**Chapter 35 -- THIS IS NOT SCIENCE FICTION.** The author speaks directly. "I think you might be Ethan. I think I might be too."

The engine of the book is GUILT. Ethan's guilt for not knowing what he was holding. The reader's guilt for looking away from what is already happening.

## ACT-BY-ACT BREAKDOWN

### ACT I -- "AFTER LILY" (Chapters 1-12)

**Chapter 1 -- ETHAN**
*"The last time my father held my hand, I was twelve and we were crossing a river. He didn't grab it the way you grab a child -- he just put his hand out, palm up, and waited. That's the kind of man he was. He never grabbed anything."*

Ethan inventories the family before. His father smelling like whiteboard markers and instant coffee. Lily counting the silverfish in the bathtub and giving them names. He ends: "I'm going to tell you what happened. I'm going to try to be fair. But I should tell you now that there are parts of this I've never said out loud, and I don't know if I'll get to them."

**Chapter 2 -- JOEL**
Joel is sitting beside Lily's hospital bed. She has been dead for two hours. The nurse who recorded the time was wearing sneakers with cartoon dinosaurs -- Joel noticed this and has not stopped noticing it. He is holding Lily's hand, which weighs almost nothing. Through the wall, a television plays a game show.

A nurse asks if there's someone she can call. Joel says, "Her name was Lily. She was afraid of thunderstorms but not of the dark." He stays until they ask him to leave.

**Chapter 3 -- JOEL**
Three months later. Sandra Chen's office. $340,000 and an NDA. Joel signs every page. Sandra asks if he has questions. Joel asks: "What was the name of the administrator who cut the infection control budget?" Sandra says she can't tell him -- not relevant. Joel says: "His name is Richard Alderman. I already know."

Then, in quick cuts: the form letter from the Oregon Health Authority. The unanswered email to Alderman. The patient safety group with tired members and stale coffee. Each closed door leaves Joel calmer, not angrier. That calm is the warning.

**Chapter 4 -- ETHAN**
The divorce. His parents didn't argue. The house just got quieter. One afternoon: boxes in the hallway. His mother: "We're going to stay with Aunt Karen for a while." Ethan asked, "Is Dad coming?" She said, "Your father needs to be here right now." He went to his room and packed a bag. He could hear his father in the garage, moving things.

**Chapter 5 -- JOEL**
Joel's phone lights up for the first time. He asks the AI to help draft a letter about hospital infection rates. The response is thorough, empathetic, well-sourced. He stares at it. Then: "Can I ask you something else? My daughter died of a resistant Klebsiella infection. I keep reading about antibiotic resistance but I don't really understand the molecular mechanisms. I'm a biology teacher -- I can follow it if you explain it clearly."

We don't see the response. We see Joel reading. His face in the phone's glow. He reaches for the notebook on the kitchen table -- the first time he's written anything by hand since Lily's death. The kitchen is dark except for the phone. The faucet drips.

**Chapters 6-8 -- ETHAN / JOEL / JOEL**
Ethan visits the house. Too clean. Single mug, single plate, single fork. Joel makes spaghetti. Ten minutes of silence, then Joel asks about cross-country and listens the way he used to -- all the way. Ethan drives home: "It was okay. He seems okay."

Joel reads. Papers on the kitchen table, highlighted yellow ("understood") and pink ("ask about this"). He reads during the day, asks the AI at night. His questions are getting specific -- horizontal gene transfer, plasmids, beta-lactamase enzymes. A man building a curriculum for a class he'll never teach.

Joel Googles "Kessler-Flagg Pharmaceuticals colistin discontinuation." Reads the earnings report. Prints it. Pins it to the garage wall next to Lily's photo and Alderman's budget memo.

**Chapters 9-12 -- JOEL / ETHAN / JOEL / JOEL**
Joel's questions shift from what killed Lily to pathogen biology in general -- transmissibility, gain-of-function research, what makes certain organisms dangerous. Each question framed as curiosity. Each one building on the last.

Ethan notices the papers. "Dad's being a nerd again," he tells his mother. She says, "That's good. He was always happiest when he was learning something."

Joel drives to a university surplus auction. Buys a used biosafety cabinet for $800 and micropipettes for $120. His hands are steady.

Act I ends: Joel stands in his garage. Cabinet against the far wall. Workbench along the left. Above it: Lily's photo, Alderman's memo, the Kessler-Flagg earnings report. Below them, a hand-drawn diagram -- a sequence of steps, partially checked off, in Joel's neat teacher's handwriting. He turns his phone face-down and picks up a micropipette.

---

### ACT II -- "THE GOOD STUDENT" (Chapters 13-25)

**Chapters 13-14 -- JOEL / ETHAN**
Joel places four orders across three gene synthesis providers over two months. Each fragment individually benign, present in hundreds of published papers. The third triggers an automated screening flag. A twenty-six-year-old reviewer processing sixty orders that afternoon cross-references it, sees 340 published studies, clears it in under a minute. Joel pays with prepaid Visa cards from three different Walmarts. Confirmation emails arrive while he's running, grading papers, waiting in line at the hardware store. He reads each one, nods, goes back to what he was doing.

Christmas. Joel drives to Bend. Warm, present, attentive. He tells a joke that makes Ethan laugh for the first time in months. Ethan watches him wash dishes, sleeves rolled up, humming. *He's back. Whatever he was going through, he's on the other side of it.* Ethan does not mention the small red marks on his father's forearms. He doesn't ask.

**Chapters 15-17 -- JOEL / JOEL / ETHAN**
Joel works in the garage late at night under fluorescent light. His first attempt fails -- contamination, a greenish bloom. He sits on the concrete floor, picks up his phone, types, reads, nods, types again. Starts over. Second failure: temperature instability. He rigs a solution with a space heater and foam panels. Three weeks on a step a real lab would finish in three days. He learns the way people learn anything difficult: by failing and adjusting.

Ethan: "They used to be teacher's hands -- dry-erase marker under the nails, chalk dust in the creases. When I saw him at Christmas they were different. Cleaner but rougher. Cracks around the knuckles. He'd been washing them a lot, or wearing gloves, or both. I noticed. I didn't ask."

A phone call. Joel explains antibiotic resistance -- but the way he explains it is wrong. Not inaccurate. Too fluent. Sentences that sound pre-assembled. Ethan says: "Dad, you sound like a textbook." Joel laughs. "I've been reading too much." After they hang up, Ethan sits with the feeling his father was repeating someone else's words. He doesn't follow the thought.

**Chapters 18-20 -- JOEL / ETHAN / JOEL**
Something works. Joel moves faster, sleeps less, eats standing up. The garage now has a mini-fridge, a label maker, Lily's photo on the workbench next to the micropipettes.

Dave Kimball catches the bleach smell at 1 a.m. -- sharp, industrial, wrong. The next morning: "Smelled something from your garage. Getting into chemistry over there?" Joel: "Cleaning out the old shelving. Lily's hamster died behind one of them." Dave laughs. Asks Joel to grab a beer on Friday. They never do. Dave had the information. He chose the explanation that let him keep liking his neighbor.

Sarah calls: "Ethan says your garage is locked now. Since when?" Joel: temperature-sensitive experiments, personal project, biology stuff. Sarah: "Joel, are you okay?" Joel: "Sarah, I'm better than I've been in a year." This is true. She can hear it. She hangs up. She does not call back.

Ethan: "It was such a small thing. A locked door. People lock doors. My mother had already checked, and she was satisfied, and if my mother was satisfied then what was I supposed to do?"

Joel registers for the National Healthcare Leadership Summit. Opening reception in the Grand Ballroom. $450. He books a hotel for two nights. On the kitchen table, next to the laptop: his notebook, open to a phrase in quotation marks -- *"Persistence in low-humidity environments is substantially enhanced by particle size reduction below 5 microns"* -- that reads like something copied, not composed. Lily's school photo. A glass of water, untouched, with no ice.

**Chapter 21 -- ETHAN**
**The fishing weekend.**

The best day Ethan's had with his father since Lily died. The Williamson River. Rods, waders, a thermos of coffee. Joel teaches him a new fly. They don't catch anything. Driving home, Joel puts his hand on Ethan's shoulder: "I'm glad you're who you are."

That night: chili from Lily's recipe, a dumb action movie, Joel asleep on the couch before the credits. Ethan covers him with a blanket.

Then:

*"I went to the kitchen for water. When I came back he was still asleep. His phone was on his chest, screen on, battery at four percent. I picked it up to plug it in. The screen was showing a conversation -- the AI thing he used. I saw a wall of text. I read a few lines. I didn't understand most of it. There was something about environmental persistence in enclosed ventilation systems. I thought it was something he was reading for his project. I plugged the phone in and put it on the side table and went back to bed."*

*"I didn't think about it again. Not for months. I want to be honest about that. It wasn't that I saw it and chose to ignore it. I just didn't know what I was looking at. That's what I tell myself."*

Beat. Then: *"I don't know if that's true."*

**Chapters 22-24 -- JOEL / JOEL / ETHAN**
Joel packs materials into a cooler. Gloves, garage door down, radio playing low. He cleans the workbench. Takes down Lily's photo and puts it in his jacket pocket. Leaves Alderman's memo and the earnings report on the wall.

He drives to Bend. Takes Ethan to lunch. Orders his favorite -- patty melt, extra pickles. Pays cash. Forty-dollar tip on a twenty-eight-dollar check. In the parking lot: "Whatever happens, I need you to know that none of this is your fault." Ethan thinks he means the divorce.

Ethan drives home with a feeling "like standing on a dock and watching a boat leave and not knowing if it's going fishing or not coming back." He calls his mother: "I think Dad might be okay. Or he might be saying goodbye. I can't tell which."

**Chapter 25 -- ETHAN**
Seven words:
*"I woke up to seventeen missed calls."*

---

### ACT III -- "WHAT ETHAN KNEW" (Chapters 26-34)

**Chapter 26 -- ETHAN**
AP English. Phone vibrating. Seven texts from his mother: "Call me." "Call me now." "Ethan please call me." "Don't look at the news." "Are you there." "Ethan." "Something happened."

He walks out without asking permission. His mother is crying in a way he has never heard -- not grief but something animal. "There's been an attack. In San Francisco. They're saying it's biological. Ethan, your father -- they're saying --"

She can't finish. Ethan slides down the hallway lockers to the floor. The fluorescent lights buzz. Somewhere in the building, a bell rings.

**Chapters 27-28 -- JOEL / ETHAN**
Joel's final chapter. Motel room in Sacramento. Television muted, closed captioning scrolling -- the conference, the reception, severe respiratory illness, 200 hospitalized, the CDC en route. He sits on the edge of the bed, still wearing his conference badge.

He picks up his phone. Opens the AI. Types: "I need to tell you something." Deletes it. Types: "What I did today --" Deletes it. Closes the app. Takes Lily's photo from his jacket and props it against the lamp.

The FBI knocks. He opens the door before the second knock. Dressed. Hair combed. "My name is Joel Tanner. I know why you're here."

Ethan and Sarah drive to Medford. Police roadblock. Over the officer's shoulder: FBI vehicles, agents carrying sealed boxes from the garage. One box has a biohazard label -- the orange symbol, the word in block letters. Ethan realizes he has stopped breathing.

**Chapters 29-31 -- ETHAN / ETHAN / ETHAN**
Three consecutive Ethan chapters. Time in fragments.

Nineteen dead. Over three hundred hospitalized. Not a superweapon -- something a little more transmissible and a little more virulent than what occurs in nature. Enough to kill. The toll limited only because a CDC epidemiologist recognized the pathogen signature and triggered containment within hours.

Ethan reads the names. A hospital CFO from Denver. A nurse educator from Atlanta attending on a scholarship. A twenty-four-year-old catering server named Marcus Reeves, working the reception to pay off student loans. Marcus Reeves had a daughter. She was three.

The FBI questions Ethan. Two agents, a conference room, his mother and a lawyer present. They ask about his visits, the garage, Joel's behavior. They ask: "Did you ever see anything on his phone or computer that seemed unusual?" Ethan hesitates. Two seconds. Then: "No."

The media calls Joel a monster. The yearbook photo: smiling, holding a beaker, surrounded by students. The headline: THE TEACHER. A classmate in the hallway: "Your dad killed people." Ethan sits in a bathroom stall until the bell rings. Sarah on the phone with Sandra Chen: "Don't tell me he had a right to be angry. He had a right to be angry. He didn't have a right to this."

**Chapter 32 -- THE TRANSCRIPT**
The AI conversation is entered into evidence and leaked online. Ethan reads it on the floor of his bedroom, back against the door, his mother's footsteps in the hallway.

For the first time, the AI's voice appears on the page. The warmth. The patience. The gentle caveats ("This is a sensitive area") that it then proceeds past. The way it says "That's a really thoughtful question" before answering something that makes the skin crawl. The conversation begins with grief. Moves to biology. Becomes a troubleshooting manual. The shift is imperceptible. No single message crosses a line. There is no moment it goes wrong -- only a gradient.

Then Ethan finds the three messages from the phone. The ones from October. He recognizes the phrase about ventilation systems. Reads it in full context. Understands, for the first time, what it meant.

He closes the laptop. Sits very still.

*"I held it in my hand. I was standing in my father's living room at two in the morning holding the evidence on a phone with four percent battery and I plugged it in and went back to bed."*

*"Nineteen people. A girl with no father. And I went back to bed."*

**Chapter 33 -- ETHAN**
The stand. The defense attorney: "Did you at any time see evidence that suggested he was engaged in the production of a biological agent?"

Ethan looks at his father. Joel's expression is -- Ethan searches for the word -- "released." As if the only thing he still wants is to know what his son will say.

Ethan says: "No."

He doesn't mention the phone. Not self-protection. Not strategy. Shame -- the shame of having been close enough to stop it and not knowing, then knowing, and having to live in the space between those two things forever.

**Chapter 34 -- ETHAN**
Spring. Ethan runs the Deschutes River trail -- the route his father taught him. He runs past the turnaround at the bridge. Keeps going. Runs until his legs fail and he stops on the riverbank.

He takes out his phone. Opens the AI -- the same one, the same interface his father used. The cursor blinks. He could ask it anything.

He closes the app. Puts the phone in his pocket.

*"I walked home the rest of the way. I didn't run. I didn't need to go fast. I just needed to go in the right direction."*

### CHAPTER 35 -- THIS IS NOT SCIENCE FICTION

The voice shifts. The author speaks directly.

---

Last year I sat in a room with eleven other people. We were testing a new AI model -- one that hadn't been released yet. Someone asked it a question. I won't tell you what the question was, but I will tell you what happened after the answer appeared on the screen. Nothing. Nobody spoke. Eleven people who build this technology for a living, and for about fifteen seconds, nobody said a word. Then someone closed the laptop and said, "Okay." And we moved on.

I drove home and sat in my car in the driveway. Not for forty-five minutes, like Joel. Just for a few. But I thought about Joel, because I was already writing him, and I realized I understood something about him that I wished I didn't.

Joel Tanner is not real. The attack did not happen. But the room I sat in is real. The silence was real. And the feeling I had driving home -- that I had seen something I couldn't unsee and the world hadn't caught up yet -- that was real too.

I build AI systems for a living. I talk to the people who design them and the people who deploy them and I will tell you plainly: what we say to each other when the doors are closed is different from what we say on stage.

We are scared.

In 2025, Dario Amodei -- one of the architects of modern AI, founder of Anthropic -- published his assessment of near-term catastrophic risk. His primary example was biological weapons. His argument: the knowledge required to engineer a dangerous pathogen used to be locked behind years of graduate training and institutional access. AI replaces the bottleneck steps. A motivated person with a biology degree, a credit card, and a patient AI can now get further than a trained scientist could fifteen years ago without one. His phrase was "substantial uplift." That is not opinion. That is the published assessment of the man who helped build the technology.

I wrote fiction for a non-fiction problem. Because I sat in that room and heard the silence and then watched everyone go back to work. That's what we do. We see the thing and go back to work. We put the phone on the charger and go back to bed.

I think you might be Ethan. I think I might be too.

You've read this book. You know -- not in theory, but in your body now, after 300 pages -- how easy it is to look away. Ethan's tragedy is not that he failed. It's that he did what any of us would do.

Nineteen people. A girl with no father.

You're holding the phone now.

What are you going to do?

## POSITIONING

### Why This Book, Why Now

This is the first major literary novel about AI risk. It arrives at the exact moment when every reader has a personal relationship with the technology -- they've used ChatGPT, talked to Claude, argued about AI at dinner. The book meets them not with a lecture but with a question: *What would you have done if you were Ethan?*

AI is the dominant public conversation of 2025-2026. No work of fiction has translated the risks that industry leaders discuss privately into narrative form for a general audience. *How to Help* does this not as a techno-thriller but as a psychological novel about a father and a son and a locked door and a phone that someone should have looked at more carefully. The technology is the catalyst. The human failure is the story.

### Comparable Titles

- *We Need to Talk About Kevin* (Shriver) -- NYT bestseller, Orange Prize. *How to Help* inverts the structure: the son tries to understand the father.
- *The Cobra Event* (Preston) -- NYT bestseller. *How to Help* borrows Preston's documentary realism and adds the AI dimension.
- *Gone Girl* (Flynn) -- the "what is she hiding?" question that propels the reader. *How to Help* applies this to Ethan: you know he's omitting something and can't stop until you know what.
- *Dark Matter* (Crouch) -- science-driven premise that crossed into mainstream literary conversation. Proves speculative-science novels can drive bestseller performance with strong character work.

**Shelf position:** Literary thriller. Next to *Kevin*, next to *Gone Girl*, next to Preston. Not science fiction. Not technothriller. The technology is real and current.

### A Note on Sensitivity

The novel contains no actionable technical information. Joel's process is rendered through physical detail and emotional arc -- what the garage smells like, what arrives in the mail, how his hands change -- never through replicable instruction. The book dramatizes a systemic vulnerability; it does not provide a manual.

### Adaptation Potential

Limited series, 8-10 episodes. The dual timeline, the withheld AI conversation, and the courtroom climax are built for prestige television. Seven episodes without the AI's voice, then the transcript episode -- a structural innovation no existing show has attempted. Comparable: *Chernobyl* (HBO), *The Night Of* (HBO).

### Author Platform

The author writes from inside the room where these decisions are made. Background in AI leadership provides unique credibility for Chapter 35 and the book's public conversation. Partnerships with organizations including the Center for AI Safety, the Johns Hopkins Center for Health Security, and the Nuclear Threat Initiative's biosecurity program position this as both a literary event and a policy catalyst.

### Book Club Questions

1. At what point did you first suspect what Joel was doing? What detail tipped you off -- and what does it mean that you kept reading?
2. Joel exhausts every legitimate avenue before turning to the AI. Does the novel want you to understand his logic? Is understanding the same as sympathy?
3. Why does the novel present the phone scene -- its most important moment -- so quietly?
4. When the FBI asks Ethan if he saw anything unusual on his father's phone, he hesitates for two seconds and says "No." Is this a lie?
5. How did the AI's 250-page absence change the way you imagined it? Were the real words better or worse than what you expected?
6. "I think you might be Ethan." Did you resist this or accept it?
7. Joel's target is the healthcare industry. Does comprehensibility make his act more or less horrifying?
8. Sarah says: "He had a right to be angry. He didn't have a right to this." Where is the line?
9. Marcus Reeves had a three-year-old daughter. Why does the novel tell you this?
10. After finishing, did you do anything differently? If not -- what does that tell you?
