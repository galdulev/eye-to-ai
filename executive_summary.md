# Executive Review Summary: Scaling Good

**Author:** Gal Dulev
**Date:** March 10, 2026
**Prepared by:** Comprehensive 15-Step Manuscript Review

---

This book is worth publishing. The core thesis — that the nonprofit sector deserves the same operational rigor as the technology industry, adapted with ethical seriousness — is timely, necessary, and well-argued. The revisions below will not change the book's identity. They will make it bulletproof.

---

## 1. THE BOOK'S 5 GREATEST STRENGTHS

**Preserve these at all costs. Any revision that weakens these has gone wrong.**

### 1. The Dignity Test (Chapter 3)
The single-question diagnostic — *"If the people your organization serves could see every internal document written about them, would they feel respected?"* — is the book's strongest claim to originality. Both the skeptical nonprofit veteran and the skeptical tech reviewer independently called it "simple, devastating." It is memorable, actionable, and genuinely new. No existing book in this space has produced anything comparable. This concept alone justifies publication.

### 2. The Founder Trap (Chapter 10)
The portrayal of the founder who built everything and now cannot leave is the most emotionally resonant chapter in the book. The veteran reviewer called it "the best I've read anywhere." It works because it names a pattern everyone recognizes but nobody discusses, and because the three-part resolution (knowledge extraction, relationship transition, governance strengthening) is concrete enough to act on immediately. The opening portrait of the exhausted founder is the best character writing in the manuscript.

### 3. The Prologue — Kulan and the Medical Clown
The dual-education framing is a masterful structural move. The cold coffee at Kulan grounds the book in lived nonprofit experience. The medical clown passage — *"the clown who walked in with attention could change the energy of an entire ward"* — is the emotional thesis of the entire book. It earns trust with nonprofit readers before introducing any tech concepts. Do not shorten or restructure this prologue.

### 4. The Organizational Debt Taxonomy (Chapter 7)
The four-type classification (process, culture, knowledge, governance debt) is a genuinely valuable adaptation of Cunningham's concept. No existing work has mapped organizational dysfunction this specifically in a nonprofit context. The Debt Inventory tool — scored by severity, cost of delay, and effort — is immediately usable by any leadership team. This chapter, together with the Founder Trap, forms the strongest consecutive section of the book.

### 5. Destroying the Overhead Myth (Chapter 8)
Both skeptical reviewers — the nonprofit veteran and the tech person — praised this chapter unprompted. The veteran said she would "photocopy it and leave it on every foundation program officer's desk." The structural critique (the overhead myth punishes investment in capacity) is not new (Dan Pallotta made this case), but the connection to organizational debt and the three-front dismantling strategy (reframe, change funder practices, board insistence) is the most complete treatment currently in print. It is the chapter most likely to be excerpted, shared, and cited.

## 2. THE 5 MOST CRITICAL REVISIONS NEEDED

### 1. Add an evidence base for your own frameworks

**Problem:** The book argues passionately for evidence-based practice but provides zero evidence that any of its 10+ original frameworks have been tested.

**Why it matters:** This is the single most damaging objection a reviewer or critic will raise. The ethical audit scored 1.5 out of 7 because the book embodies the gap it diagnoses — launching at full scale before validating.

**What to do:** Add a transparent "How This Framework Was Developed" section (1-2 pages, possibly in the introduction or as a brief author's note). Acknowledge which concepts have been field-tested with consulting clients, which are synthesized from existing research, and which are theoretical proposals. Where possible, add 2-3 specific examples of organizations that used elements of the Impact Stack. If no formal validation exists, say so honestly — the book's own principles demand it.

**Estimated effort:** 1-2 days

### 2. Cite your intellectual debts — especially Ann Mei Chang and Dan Pallotta

**Problem:** The book does not cite *Lean Impact* (Ann Mei Chang, 2018), which already applied Lean Startup to the social sector comprehensively, or Dan Pallotta's work on the overhead myth. This is the highest competitive-landscape risk.

**Why it matters:** Knowledgeable readers will notice. Reviewers will notice. The omission makes the book look either uninformed or deliberately uncrediting. It undermines the authority the book needs.

**What to do:** Add 4-6 citations to key predecessors. Position the book explicitly as the first *unified operating system* — a synthesis that integrates what Chang, Pallotta, Kania & Kramer, IDEO, and others have addressed in isolation. Credit generously. Lead with what's genuinely original (the Dignity Test, the Organizational Debt taxonomy, the full-stack integration). A brief "Standing on Shoulders" paragraph in the introduction would accomplish this elegantly.

**Estimated effort:** 3-4 hours

### 3. Fix factual inaccuracies

**Problem:** The Amazon profitability claim (Chapter 8: "not profitable for its first twenty years") is verifiably wrong — Amazon's first full-year profit was 2003, approximately nine years after founding. The November 9, 2010 software bug (Chapter 5) cannot be identified and appears to be a composite. The "midwestern state" child welfare algorithm (Chapter 6) resembles the Allegheny County, Pennsylvania case (not midwestern, launched 2016 not 2019). The "thirty percent" underpay claim in the Prologue is unsourced — documented gaps are 4-18%.

**Why it matters:** A book demanding evidence-based rigor cannot contain easily disproven claims. Each factual error gives skeptics permission to dismiss the entire argument.

**What to do:** Correct Amazon to "approximately nine years." Either identify the actual software incident or disclose it as illustrative/composite. Verify and correct the child welfare algorithm details. Source or soften the salary gap claim.

**Estimated effort:** 2-3 hours

### 4. Address racial equity — the silence is conspicuous

**Problem:** The words "racism," "DEI," "equity," "anti-racism," and "racial justice" do not appear anywhere in the manuscript. In a 2026 book about nonprofit effectiveness in America, this absence will be noticed and criticized.

**Why it matters:** Racial equity is not a peripheral issue for American nonprofits — it is central to program design, staffing, community trust, data ethics, and systems change. The gap analysis identified this as the single most critical missing topic.

**What to do:** This does not require a new chapter. Add 2-3 paragraphs distributed across the most relevant existing chapters: Chapter 3 (participatory design and power dynamics), Chapter 6 (algorithmic bias and data — the child welfare example already touches this), and Chapter 17 (systems change). Acknowledge how structural racism shapes the problems nonprofits address and the communities they serve. Be specific, not performative.

**Estimated effort:** 4-6 hours

### 5. Disclose the status of anonymous anecdotes

**Problem:** Nine of ten social-sector anecdotes are anonymous with no disclosure of whether they are real, anonymized, or composite. The $3M youth center (Ch.2), the housing program (Ch.3), and the East Africa maternal health organization (Ch.11) read as real case studies but cannot be verified.

**Why it matters:** Readers who try to look up these stories and fail will lose trust. A book demanding transparency from nonprofits must model it.

**What to do:** Add a brief author's note disclosing the methodology: "Names and identifying details have been changed to protect organizational privacy. Some examples are composites drawn from multiple organizations." Where stories are based on real organizations that have consented to be named, name them — named case studies are dramatically more credible than anonymous ones.

**Estimated effort:** 1-2 hours

## 3. THE 3 STRONGEST SKEPTICAL OBJECTIONS AND SUGGESTED RESPONSES

### Objection 1: "You built 10 original frameworks with 40+ tools and provided zero evidence any of them work — aren't you doing exactly what you criticize?"

**Why it's dangerous:** This is the most intellectually devastating objection because it uses the book's own logic against it. Any hostile reviewer will find it. It cannot be deflected — only defused.

**How to respond:** The book should preemptively address this in the introduction or conclusion. Acknowledge the tension directly: the Impact Stack is a synthesis of principles that have been individually validated (Lean Startup methodology, participatory design, outcome measurement) but have not been tested as an integrated framework. Invite the reader to treat the book itself as a Minimum Viable Program — a testable hypothesis, not a finished product. Frame this as intellectual honesty, not weakness. The author could add: *"I wrote this book to start a conversation, not to end one. If I have followed my own principles, this framework should be subjected to the same scrutiny I ask you to apply to your programs."* This turns the vulnerability into a strength.

### Objection 2: "This is tech condescension — you compare best-case tech against worst-case nonprofit, and every comparison runs one direction."

**Why it's dangerous:** The tone analysis found 9 instances of "the non-profit sector has no / is terrible at / has never" construction and zero instances where the nonprofit sector is credited with understanding something before tech did. The Prologue romanticizes tech culture ("without ego") while the sector's surveillance capitalism, diversity failures, and product disasters go unmentioned. Nonprofit readers who feel talked down to will stop reading.

**How to respond:** Two surgical fixes. First, add 3-4 passages where the nonprofit sector's wisdom *precedes* tech — community organizing understood user research before Silicon Valley had a name for it; nonprofits practiced mission-driven culture decades before tech companies started writing values statements; the sector's ethical instincts about data and AI are ahead of tech, not behind. Second, add one paragraph early in the book acknowledging that tech's track record includes catastrophic failures (move fast and break things, surveillance capitalism, algorithmic bias) and that the book is importing *discipline*, not *culture*. The Prologue already gestures at this — expand it by two sentences.

### Objection 3: "This framework claims to serve 'every nonprofit leader' but is really calibrated for $500K-$5M organizations with 5-30 staff."

**Why it's dangerous:** A two-person grassroots organization cannot run Debt Inventory sprints. A $50M hospital foundation doesn't need the Funding Stack introduction. The mismatch between claimed audience and actual audience will alienate the majority of readers who fall outside the sweet spot.

**How to respond:** Name the sweet spot honestly. Add a paragraph in the introduction: *"This book is written primarily for leaders of nonprofits in a growth phase — organizations with budgets between roughly $500K and $5M, teams of 5-30 people, hitting the ceiling of founder-centric, informal operations. If you run a smaller organization, the principles apply but the implementation will look different. If you lead a large institution, you likely have some of these systems already — this book can help you identify which ones are missing."* This costs nothing, gains enormous credibility, and actually helps readers self-select.

## 4. RECOMMENDED REVISION SEQUENCE

### Round 1: Credibility Foundations (Days 1-2)
*Highest impact per hour — these changes address the objections that could sink a review.*

1. Fix all factual inaccuracies (Amazon, software bug, algorithm, salary gap) — **2-3 hours**
2. Add author's note on anecdote methodology (real/anonymized/composite) — **1 hour**
3. Add intellectual debt citations (Chang, Pallotta, Kania & Kramer, IDEO) — **3-4 hours**
4. Add "How This Framework Was Developed" transparency section — **3-4 hours**

### Round 2: Substance Gaps (Days 3-5)
*Fills the holes that reviewers and serious readers will find.*

5. Address racial equity (3 paragraphs across Ch.3, Ch.6, Ch.17) — **4-6 hours**
6. Add "Where This Framework Breaks" section with boundary conditions and counter-examples — **4-6 hours**
7. Reframe the Impact Stack from "strict prerequisites, cannot skip" to "strong mutual reinforcements with a general bottom-up bias" — **3-4 hours**
8. Name the sweet-spot audience explicitly in the introduction — **1 hour**

### Round 3: Tone and Polish (Days 6-8)
*Transforms the book from good to excellent.*

9. Add 3-4 passages where nonprofit knowledge precedes tech knowledge — **2-3 hours**
10. Soften or qualify the 9 "nonprofit sector has never / is terrible at" constructions — **2-3 hours**
11. Add brief staff mental health / burnout acknowledgment in Ch.9 or Ch.10 — **1-2 hours**
12. Preemptively address the "practicing what you preach" objection in the introduction or conclusion — **1-2 hours**
13. Add endnotes section (25-30 references for key claims) — **4-6 hours**

## 5. THE MINIMUM VIABLE REVISION

**If you have only 2 days before the manuscript is due, do these 10 things — in this order.**

- [ ] **Fix the Amazon claim** (Ch.8): Change "first twenty years" to "nearly a decade." — *30 minutes*

- [ ] **Add anecdote disclosure note** (front matter): One paragraph stating that names/details have been changed and some examples are composites. — *30 minutes*

- [ ] **Cite Ann Mei Chang and Dan Pallotta** (introduction + Ch.8): Two sentences in the introduction acknowledging *Lean Impact* and Pallotta's overhead myth work. One footnote in Ch.8. — *45 minutes*

- [ ] **Name your audience** (introduction): Add one paragraph defining the sweet spot ($500K-$5M, growth-phase orgs) and acknowledging that smaller/larger orgs will need to adapt. — *30 minutes*

- [ ] **Add the "practicing what I preach" preemption** (conclusion): 2-3 sentences acknowledging the framework hasn't been formally validated and inviting readers to test it. — *45 minutes*

- [ ] **Fix the child welfare algorithm details** (Ch.6): Verify source and correct geography/date, or add a footnote acknowledging the composite nature. — *30 minutes*

- [ ] **Source or soften the salary gap claim** (Prologue): Either cite a source for "thirty percent" or change to "significantly less" with a footnote. — *30 minutes*

- [ ] **Add one "nonprofits got there first" passage** (Ch.3 or Ch.9): One paragraph crediting the nonprofit sector's pioneering work in participatory design or community organizing. — *45 minutes*

- [ ] **Add two sentences on racial equity** (Ch.6, during the algorithmic bias discussion): Acknowledge that structural racism shapes data, communities, and program design. — *30 minutes*

- [ ] **Add 8-10 endnote references** for the most critical unsourced claims (donor attrition rate, overhead ratio letter, tech debt origin, salary gap data). — *2 hours*

**Total: approximately 7-8 hours across 2 days.**

These ten changes will not make the book perfect. They will make it defensible. They address the factual errors that could embarrass you, the attribution gaps that could alienate peers, and the self-awareness gaps that could arm hostile critics. Everything else — the racial equity expansion, the boundary conditions section, the full tone pass — can be done in a second round.

---

**Final note:** This book has something most manuscripts don't — a genuine contribution to how people think about nonprofit effectiveness. The Dignity Test, the Organizational Debt taxonomy, the Founder Trap diagnosis, and the full-stack integration are real additions to the field. The revisions above are not about fixing a broken book. They are about protecting a good book from the preventable criticisms that could keep it from reaching the people who need it.

Build the evidence. Credit your predecessors. Name your limits. The book already has the heart. Give it the armor.
